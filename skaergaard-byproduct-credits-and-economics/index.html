Skaergaard Byproduct Credits: How Gold and Rhodium Drive Project Economics

One of Skaergaard's most underappreciated advantages is its byproduct credits. While the project is often discussed as a palladium deposit, the gold and rhodium contributions to project economics are substantial enough to shift the investment case meaningfully.

The Byproduct Credit Mechanism

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

In mining economics, byproduct credits reduce the net cost of producing the primary metal. If a mine produces palladium as its main product but also recovers gold and rhodium, the revenue from gold and rhodium offsets the cost of mining and processing, reducing the effective cost per palladium ounce.

The math is straightforward: if total mining and processing costs are US$50 per tonne of ore, and the ore produces 2 grams of palladium, 1 gram of gold, and 0.02 grams of rhodium per tonne, the revenue from gold and rhodium directly reduces the cost attributable to palladium production.

Gold Contribution

Skaergaard's gold resource of 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces is significant in its own right. At a gold price of US$5,100 per ounce (Feb 2026), the in-situ gold value exceeds US$120 billion, comparable to many standalone gold deposits that attract major mining investment.

The gold contribution to Skaergaard's economics works on multiple levels:

  • Direct revenue: Gold recovered during processing is sold at market prices, contributing directly to cash flow
  • Cost offset: Gold revenue reduces the net cost per PGM ounce recovered
  • Diversification: If palladium prices decline, gold revenue provides a partial hedge
  • Investor appeal: Gold attracts a different class of investor than PGMs alone, broadening the potential shareholder base

Gold prices above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026), supported by central bank purchases (1,045 tonnes in 2024), jewelry demand, and investment flows, provide a favorable environment for Skaergaard's gold economics. If gold prices remain elevated, the byproduct credit could reduce Skaergaard's net PGM costs by US$150-300 per ounce depending on the gold-to-PGM ratio in the processing stream.

Rhodium Contribution

Rhodium is the highest-value PGM per ounce, typically trading at several multiples of palladium or platinum. Skaergaard's rhodium content, while small in absolute tonnage, is valuable enough to materially impact project economics.

Key characteristics of rhodium that matter for Skaergaard:

  • Price: Rhodium has traded between US$4,000 and US$30,000 per ounce in recent years, making it the most valuable PGM
  • Supply concentration: Global rhodium supply is extremely concentrated, with South Africa and Russia accounting for the vast majority
  • Demand: Automotive catalytic converters for gasoline engines are the primary demand driver
  • Volatility: Rhodium prices are highly volatile, creating both upside potential and downside risk

Even a modest rhodium byproduct credit of US$50-100 per PGM ounce would significantly improve Skaergaard's economics. At higher rhodium prices, the contribution could be even more substantial.

Quantifying the Byproduct Impact

While exact figures depend on metallurgical recovery rates and the specific gold-to-PGM ratio in different mining zones, a reasonable estimate of the byproduct contribution to Skaergaard's economics is:

  • Gold credit: US$150-300 per PGM ounce at US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026) gold
  • Rhodium credit: US$50-100 per PGM ounce at US$5,000/oz rhodium
  • Combined: US$200-400 per PGM ounce reduction in net production cost

If Skaergaard's all-in sustaining cost is US$1,800 (Feb 2026) per PGM ounce before byproduct credits, the after-credit cost could fall to US$500-700 per ounce. This is competitive with the lowest-cost South African producers and significantly below the industry average.

Comparison with PGM-Only Projects

Projects that produce only PGMs without significant byproduct credits face a different economic reality. For example, a PGM-only project with an AISC of US$1,800 (Feb 2026) per ounce has no revenue diversification and is fully exposed to PGM price movements. Skaergaard's gold and rhodium credits provide:

  • Lower breakeven palladium price: The price at which Skaergaard covers its costs is lower than for PGM-only projects
  • Margin cushion: Higher-margin periods (when PGM prices are elevated) generate more cash flow
  • Resilience: During PGM price downturns, gold and rhodium revenue partially offset the impact

Sensitivity to Metal Prices

The byproduct credit makes Skaergaard's economics sensitive to gold and rhodium prices in addition to palladium:

  • If gold rises to US$2,500/oz: Additional US$50-75 per ounce credit improvement
  • If gold falls to US$1,500/oz: Credit reduction of US$75-100 per ounce
  • If rhodium spikes: Significant but temporary improvement in project economics
  • If rhodium collapses: Modest reduction in economics due to smaller absolute contribution

The net effect is that Skaergaard benefits from the current strong gold price environment while being somewhat insulated from palladium-specific volatility.
