Skaergaard Tailings and Waste Management: Arctic-Specific Challenges

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardtailingswaste managementenvironmentalgrml

Tailings and waste management are among the most critical environmental and engineering challenges for any mining operation. For Skaergaard in East Greenland, the Arctic environment adds layers of complexity that require specialized approaches to tailings storage, water management, and site rehabilitation.

Tailings Volume and Character

Skaergaard's planned processing rate of 3 million tonnes per annum will generate approximately 2.5-2.8 million tonnes of tailings annually. The tailings will consist of finely ground rock material from which PGMs, gold, and other metals have been extracted through flotation.

Key characteristics of Skaergaard tailings:

  • **Particle size**: Flotation tailings are typically silt to fine sand in particle size, with a significant fraction of clay-sized material
  • **Sulfide content**: PGM ores often contain sulfide minerals (pyrite, pentlandite, chalcopyrite) that can generate acid when exposed to air and water
  • **Water content**: Tailings are typically deposited as a slurry containing 30-40% water by weight
  • **Residual metals**: Tailings contain trace amounts of PGMs and other metals that were not recovered during processing

    • Tailings Storage Facility Design

    The tailings storage facility (TSF) is one of the largest infrastructure components of the Skaergaard project. Design considerations include:

    Location: The TSF should be located as close to the processing plant as practical to minimize pumping costs, while meeting environmental requirements for distance from waterways and sensitive areas. In East Greenland's topography, a valley or natural depression near the mine site would be the preferred location.

    Construction method: Two primary approaches are considered:

  • **Upstream raised embankment**: Tailings are deposited behind a starter dam, and the embankment is progressively raised using compacted tailings. This is the most common method globally but requires careful management in permafrost conditions
  • **Downstream raised embankment**: The embankment is raised downstream using borrow material, providing greater stability. This method is preferred for larger dams and higher-consequence settings

    • Liner system: A geomembrane liner may be required to prevent seepage into groundwater, particularly if acid-generating sulfides are present in the tailings.

    Permafrost Challenges

    Permafrost at Skaergaard creates unique tailings management challenges:

  • **Foundation stability**: Permafrost provides a stable foundation for the TSF if kept frozen, but thawing can cause differential settlement and embankment instability
  • **Thawing from above**: The TSF embankment and tailings surface are exposed to air temperatures that may fluctuate above and below freezing, creating an active layer of thawed material
  • **Thawing from below**: The warm tailings slurry (which exits the processing plant at above-freezing temperatures) can thaw underlying permafrost
  • **Water management**: Thawing permafrost can release water that was previously trapped in ice, increasing seepage and pore pressures

    • Mitigation strategies include insulating the tailings surface with waste rock, using cold tailings deposition techniques, and designing the embankment for post-thaw conditions.

    Water Treatment

    Water management at Skaergaard involves several treatment and recycling systems:

  • **Process water recycling**: A closed-circuit water system that recycles water from the processing plant back to the grinding circuit, minimizing freshwater consumption
  • **Tailings water recovery**: Water that drains from the tailings facility is collected and returned to the processing plant
  • **Contact water treatment**: Water that has been in contact with tailings or waste rock may require treatment for suspended solids, dissolved metals, and pH adjustment before discharge
  • **Non-contact water management**: Clean surface water (rainfall, snowmelt) is diverted around waste facilities to minimize the volume of water requiring treatment

    • In Arctic conditions, water treatment faces additional challenges: biological treatment processes are less efficient at low temperatures, and freezing conditions can damage treatment infrastructure.

    Waste Rock Management

    In addition to tailings, the open-pit mining operation will generate significant volumes of waste rock. Waste rock management includes:

  • **Waste rock dump design**: Engineered dump configurations that maintain stability and minimize environmental impact
  • **Acid rock drainage (ARD) management**: Waste rock that contains sulfide minerals may generate acid when exposed to air and water. ARD testing of waste rock is required to determine whether special handling is needed
  • **Selective placement**: Potentially acid-generating waste rock may be placed in specific locations within the dump to facilitate long-term management and water collection
  • **Progressive reclamation**: Reclaiming completed dump surfaces as mining progresses, rather than waiting until mine closure

    • Site Rehabilitation and Closure

    Greenland's regulatory framework requires a decommissioning plan and financial assurance for site rehabilitation. Key closure considerations include:

  • **TSF closure**: The tailings facility must be covered and vegetated (where possible) to prevent erosion and dust generation. Long-term water management may be required for decades after closure
  • **Open-pit closure**: The open pit may be allowed to fill with water (forming a pit lake) or backfilled with waste rock, depending on the closure plan
  • **Infrastructure removal**: Processing plant, camp, and other infrastructure are dismantled and removed
  • **Land use**: Post-mining land use objectives are defined in consultation with regulators and communities

    • Financial assurance (bonding) for closure costs is typically required before mining permits are issued. The amount is based on detailed closure cost estimates and is held by the government as a guarantee that funds are available for rehabilitation regardless of the company's financial condition.

    Conclusion

    Tailings and waste management at Skaergaard must account for Arctic-specific conditions including permafrost, freezing temperatures, and remote location. While these challenges add complexity and cost, they are manageable with proper engineering design and ongoing monitoring. The environmental standards required by Greenland's regulatory framework ensure that Skaergaard's waste management practices will meet or exceed international best practices.
