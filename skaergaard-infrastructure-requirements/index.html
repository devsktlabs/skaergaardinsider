Skaergaard Infrastructure Requirements: Building a Mine from Scratch in East Greenland

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardinfrastructurelogisticsgrml

Developing a mining operation in East Greenland means building infrastructure that exists elsewhere as a given. There are no existing roads, no power grid, no port facilities, and no trained mining workforce within commuting distance of the Skaergaard site. This infrastructure deficit is both the project's greatest challenge and its greatest capital cost driver.

Access and Transportation

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

The Skaergaard deposit is located approximately 60 kilometers inland from the East Greenland coast. Getting ore to a processing facility and concentrates to market requires a complete transportation system:

  • Haul road: An all-weather or seasonal access road connecting the mine site to the coast, capable of supporting heavy equipment transport during construction and ore/concentrate haulage during operations
  • Port facility: A marine terminal on the coast capable of receiving bulk carrier vessels for concentrate export and supply deliveries. The fjord system in East Greenland provides natural deep-water harbors that reduce port construction requirements
  • Air transport: An airstrip for personnel transport and emergency medical evacuation. The remote location makes air access essential for safety and operational flexibility

The haul road is the single largest infrastructure item. Constructing 60 kilometers of mining road in Arctic terrain is a significant engineering challenge involving permafrost management, stream crossings, and grade control.

Power Supply

Greenland has no centralized power grid near Skaergaard. The project must generate its own electricity:

  • Initial phase: Diesel generators are the most straightforward power source for construction and early operations, despite high fuel costs and carbon intensity
  • Long-term options: Hydroelectric power is the most attractive long-term option, as East Greenland has significant hydro potential from glacial meltwater rivers. Wind power is another possibility given the consistent wind patterns in the region. A hybrid diesel-renewable system could reduce fuel costs and emissions over time
  • Power demand: A 3 million tonne per annum processing operation would require approximately 20-30 megawatts of installed power capacity

Fuel delivery represents a recurring logistics cost. Diesel must be shipped to the port facility and trucked to the mine site, with seasonal shipping windows constraining delivery schedules.

Water Supply

Processing PGM ores requires significant water for grinding, flotation, and tailings management. Skaergaard's proximity to glacial meltwater sources provides water availability, but the infrastructure to capture, store, and distribute water must be built:

  • Water intake: Capture structures on glacial streams
  • Storage: Reservoirs or tanks sized to handle seasonal flow variations
  • Distribution: Pumping and piping systems connecting water sources to the processing plant
  • Recycling: Process water recycling systems to minimize freshwater consumption and reduce environmental discharge

Processing Plant

The processing plant is the operational core of the project. For a PGM operation, the plant typically includes:

  • Primary crushing: Reducing run-of-mine ore to a size suitable for grinding
  • Grinding circuit: SAG mill and ball mills to liberate PGM minerals from the host rock
  • Flotation circuit: Multi-stage flotation to produce a PGM concentrate and a gold concentrate
  • Concentrate handling: Storage, drying, and loading facilities for shipping
  • Tailings management: Tailings storage facility with water management systems

Estimated processing plant capital cost is US$150-250 million, representing a significant portion of total project capital.

Worker Accommodation and Camp

Skaergaard's remote location requires a self-contained worker camp:

  • Accommodation: Dormitory-style housing for 300-500 workers during construction, scaling to 200-300 during operations
  • Catering: Kitchen and dining facilities capable of serving the full workforce
  • Medical: On-site clinic with emergency stabilization capability and telemedicine links
  • Recreation: Recreation facilities for mental health and retention in an isolated environment
  • Utilities: Water treatment, sewage treatment, waste management, and communication systems

Fly-in-fly-out (FIFO) rostering is the standard approach for remote Arctic operations, with workers typically on 2-4 week rotations.

Communications

Reliable communications are essential for both operations and safety:

  • Satellite communications: Primary data and voice connectivity
  • Radio systems: Site-wide radio network for operational coordination
  • Weather monitoring: On-site meteorological stations for safety and planning

Environmental Infrastructure

Arctic mining requires environmental infrastructure that may not be standard in temperate locations:

  • Permafrost monitoring: Instrumentation to track permafrost conditions around the mine site
  • Water quality monitoring: Real-time monitoring stations on streams and marine receiving environments
  • Dust suppression: Systems to control fugitive dust from mining and processing operations
  • Wildlife management: Protocols for minimizing wildlife interaction with operations

Cost Implications

The infrastructure requirement is the primary reason why Arctic mining projects require significantly more capital than comparable projects in established mining regions:

  • Road and port: US$100-200 million
  • Power: US$50-100 million (including fuel storage and distribution)
  • Processing plant: US$150-250 million
  • Camp and facilities: US$50-80 million
  • Environmental and permitting: US$20-40 million
  • Total infrastructure (excluding mine development): US$370-670 million

This infrastructure represents a sunk cost that benefits any future expansion or additional mineral development in the area. The road, port, and power systems built for Skaergaard could serve other deposits in the region, potentially attracting further investment.

Conclusion

The infrastructure challenge at Skaergaard is substantial but not unprecedented. Oil and gas operations in the Arctic have demonstrated that large-scale remote infrastructure can be built and operated successfully. The key is careful planning, phased development to manage capital requirements, and engineering solutions adapted to Arctic conditions. The infrastructure built for Skaergaard will have lasting value for Greenland's broader resource development ambitions.
