Skaergaard Environmental Assessment and Permitting Pathway

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardenvironmentalpermittingEIA

Greenland's environmental regulatory framework requires a comprehensive environmental impact assessment (EIA) before mining permits can be issued. For Skaergaard, the EIA process will be a critical path item that determines the project timeline and shapes operating conditions. Understanding this process is essential for investors tracking development milestones.

Greenland's Regulatory Framework

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Greenland's mineral resources are governed by the Mineral Resources Act, which establishes the framework for exploration, development, and production. Key elements include:

  • Environmental impact assessment: Required for all mining projects above a threshold size, which Skaergaard will clearly exceed
  • Social impact assessment: Required to evaluate the project's effects on local communities, employment, and cultural resources
  • Biodiversity assessment: Required to evaluate effects on flora, fauna, and ecosystems
  • Decommissioning plan: Required to ensure proper site closure and rehabilitation after mining ceases

The EIA process is administered by the Government of Greenland's Department of Mineral Resources in coordination with environmental authorities.

The EIA Process

The EIA for Skaergaard will involve several phases:

  • Scoping: Defining the scope of environmental studies required, including the geographic area of influence, the environmental parameters to be assessed, and the methodology to be used
  • Baseline studies: Comprehensive data collection on environmental conditions before mining begins. This includes water quality, air quality, vegetation, wildlife populations, fish populations, marine ecosystems, noise levels, and visual impact
  • Impact prediction: Modeling the project's predicted effects on each environmental parameter, including direct effects (from mining and processing) and indirect effects (from infrastructure, workforce, and supply chain)
  • Mitigation measures: Identifying measures to avoid, minimize, or compensate for predicted environmental effects
  • Public consultation: Opportunities for public comment and community input on the EIA findings
  • Decision: The Greenland government reviews the EIA and makes a permitting decision, which may include conditions that the project must meet

Key Environmental Considerations for Skaergaard

Several environmental factors are particularly relevant for Skaergaard's Arctic location:

Permafrost and ground ice: Mining and infrastructure development can disturb permafrost, potentially causing ground subsidence, changes to hydrology, and accelerated thawing. Permafrost management plans must address these risks.

Water resources: The processing plant will require water for grinding and flotation, and tailings management must prevent contamination of surface and groundwater. Glacial meltwater streams in the project area are ecologically sensitive.

Marine environment: The port facility and concentrate shipping operations will affect the marine environment. Oil spill prevention, ballast water management, and underwater noise from shipping must be managed.

Wildlife: East Greenland supports populations of muskox, reindeer, Arctic fox, and various bird species. Mining operations must minimize habitat disturbance and wildlife interaction.

Air quality: Diesel power generation and mining operations will produce emissions including particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, and sulfur oxides. Air quality monitoring and management plans are required.

Visual impact: Open-pit mining and associated infrastructure will alter the landscape. Visual impact assessment is relevant because of the wilderness values of the East Greenland coast.

The Citronen Fjord Precedent

Citronen Fjord, Greenland's most advanced mining project, received environmental approval in 2019. This provides a valuable precedent for Skaergaard:

  • The approval demonstrates that Greenland's regulatory system can process large-scale mining EIAs
  • The conditions attached to the Citronen Fjord approval provide insight into likely conditions for Skaergaard
  • The timeline from EIA submission to approval at Citronen Fjord (approximately 2-3 years) provides a rough benchmark for Skaergaard's timeline

Timeline Expectations

Based on the Citronen Fjord precedent and comparable Arctic mining projects, the EIA process for Skaergaard is expected to take 2-4 years from initiation to final approval:

  • Year 1-2: Baseline studies and data collection
  • Year 2-3: Impact assessment and EIA report preparation
  • Year 3-4: Public consultation and government review

The EIA runs in parallel with other pre-feasibility and feasibility work, so it does not necessarily extend the overall development timeline if managed effectively.

Conditions and Compliance

The environmental permit for Skaergaard will likely include conditions requiring:

  • Ongoing environmental monitoring and reporting
  • Financial assurance (bonding) for site rehabilitation
  • Limits on emissions, water discharge, and waste disposal
  • Wildlife management protocols
  • Community benefit agreements
  • Progressive reclamation of disturbed areas

Compliance with these conditions adds to operating costs but also creates a framework for responsible environmental management that can support the project's social license.

Conclusion

The EIA process is a necessary and manageable part of Skaergaard's development. The Citronen Fjord precedent demonstrates that Greenland's regulatory system is functional and that environmental approval is achievable for well-designed projects. The key is conducting thorough baseline studies, proposing robust mitigation measures, and engaging transparently with regulators and communities. For investors, the EIA milestone is a de-risking event that, once achieved, removes a significant source of development uncertainty.
