The metallurgy problem for PGM in layered intrusions

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.
PGM ores in layered mafic intrusions are rarely “metals in solution.” The dominant reality is that:

  • Pd, Pt, and often Rh are hosted in discrete mineral phases
  • Those hosts are commonly associated with sulfides and, secondarily, with specific silicate textures
  • Liberation depends on grind size and sulfide exposure

As a result, “recovery optimization” means controlling:

  • Liberation (comminution circuit and grind target)
  • Selective concentration (beneficiation to reduce mass and upgrade sulfide/PGM density)
  • PGM separation (processing of PGM-bearing concentrates)
  • Metallurgical variability (different zones behaving differently)

Benchmark process structure (industry pattern)

While each deposit is unique, most commercial PGM processes share a modular structure:

1. Beneficiation

  • Crushing and grinding
  • Flotation to concentrate sulfides and PGM-associated minerals

    • 2. Concentrate treatment

  • Further conditioning and regrind if needed
  • Treatment via smelting or pressure oxidation (depending on sulfide type) and downstream refining

    • 3. PGM recovery

  • Separation of PGMs from impurities through metallurgical routes

    • The key plant engineering question is how much of the beneficiation burden is carried by flotation versus downstream treatment.

    Comminution: the liberation dial

    In PGM ores, grind size impacts:

    • Degree of liberation of PGM host minerals
    • Production of slimes that can hurt flotation selectivity
    • Cost and wear in milling

    Optimization approach typically focuses on:

    • Establishing a target grind (e.g., P80) that liberates PGM hosts without generating excessive fine material
    • Performing locked-cycle tests across representative ore composites

    Key test outputs for process design:

    • Liberation data (microscopy, QEMSCAN/MLA style mineralogy where available)
    • Flotation response curves versus grind
    • Sensitivity of concentrate grades and mass pull to grind

    Beneficiation: sulfide flotation as the workhorse

    If Skaergaard mineralization is sulfide-associated (common in layered mafic PGM systems), flotation is usually the primary concentration step.

    Common flotation objectives

    A flotation stage aims to:

    • Maximize sulfide recovery (which often correlates with PGM recovery)
    • Produce a concentrate with sufficiently high tenor to make downstream separation efficient
    • Maintain tailings quality (avoid gangue entrainment)

    Key variables to control

    • Collector type and dosage
    • pH control and depressant strategy
    • Aeration and froth stability
    • Residence time and agitation

    Why mineralogy dominates

    If Pd and Pt hosts sit inside specific sulfide species or intergrowths, flotation selectivity depends on:

    • Whether those sulfides float well under the chosen chemistry
    • Whether any PGM is liberated into silicates before flotation

    Optimization often uses testwork to quantify the relationship between:

    • Sulfide mass pull
    • Pd/Pt grades in concentrate
    • Overall recovery

    Concentrate polishing: multi-stage flotation logic

    Many PGM plants use multiple rougher-cleaner stages to:

    • Reduce gangue in the final concentrate
    • Improve concentrate tenor
    • Control variability

    Polishing flotation is also a hedge against zone-to-zone variability.

    Practical design principle:

    • If one ore zone floats “too broadly,” downstream costs can rise due to impurity loading.
    • If another zone floats weakly, the plant may need regrind or chemistry adjustments.

    Downstream PGM recovery: refining logic in brief

    Once you have a PGM-bearing concentrate, the downstream route separates PGMs from a matrix of:

    • Nickel/copper/iron
    • Silicates and residual gangue
    • Other PGMs and trace elements

    Industry routes vary, but the technical constraints are consistent:

    • Smelting is energetic but can handle certain sulfide chemistries well
    • Pressure oxidation is valuable when sulfides are refractory to conventional treatment

    Refining steps then focus on selective precipitation and solvent extraction-like chemistry to isolate Pd/Pt/Rh.

    For investors and planners, the most useful optimization lens is:

    • What is the expected metallurgical recovery of PdEq to marketable product?
    • What is the loss distribution across tailings, intermediate streams, and refining residue?

    Recovery optimization under variable feed

    A project at 300,000 to 500,000 PGM oz/year peak production cannot rely on “single-point” metallurgy.

    Optimization must include feed variability:

    • Different stratigraphic horizons
    • Seasonal moisture and weathering effects on run-of-mine characteristics
    • Mineralogical heterogeneity between phases

    Engineering and metallurgists typically address this through:

    • Blending strategies (phase-based and stockpile-based)
    • Adaptive process control (chemistry adjustments)
    • Periodic testwork refresh cycles

    Tailings streams are not “waste,” they are data

    Even when flotation is successful, process engineers quantify:

    • Pd and Pt losses into tailings
    • Copper/nickel losses that might indicate depressed sulfides

    Tailings assay and mineralogy can reveal whether losses are:

    • True mineralogical losses (PGM hosts locked in gangue)
    • Or operational losses (chemistry, grind, or froth behavior)

    That drives whether to change:

    • Comminution settings
    • Flotation reagents and pH
    • Collector regime

    What “optimization” looks like on a test program

    A robust metallurgical test program usually includes:

    • Multiple ore composites across zones
    • Grind sensitivity series
    • Flotation performance curves and regrind tests
    • Mass balance across rougher-cleaner concentrates
    • Recoveries for each key metal

    For PGM projects, the most informative outputs are:

    • Recovery curves versus grind and reagent dosage
    • Concentrate tenor and mass pull
    • Metallurgical balance sheet: feed to concentrate to tailings

    Translating metallurgy into financial outcomes

    Conceptual AISC of US$800 to 1,100 per PGM ounce is only credible if metallurgy supports:

    • High recoveries (minimizing payable losses)
    • Stable throughput (recoveries are often throughput-sensitive)
    • Controllable reagent consumption

    Optimization also affects cost through:

    • Mill throughput versus grind target
    • Reagent demand and reagent recovery
    • Water balance and conditioning costs

    Diligence checklist: what to verify

    To evaluate a Skaergaard metallurgy and recovery story, focus on:

    • Which ore domains were tested (and whether composites represent the planned mine schedule)
    • The achieved recoveries for Pd, Pt, and Rh (and how those map to PdEq)
    • Concentrate specs that determine downstream refining yields
    • Evidence of robustness under variability (not just one composite)
    • Time-dependent effects (e.g., reagent performance and plant ramp-up targets)

    Bottom line

    Recovery optimization for Skaergaard is a chain: stratigraphy-controlled mineralogy dictates comminution needs, comminution dictates flotation liberation and selectivity, and flotation dictates concentrate quality for downstream PGM separation. The project’s conceptual cost range only holds when metallurgy is resilient across zones and phases.
