Skaergaard Open-Pit vs Underground: The Mining Method Decision

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

The choice between open-pit and underground mining is one of the most consequential decisions in any mining project's development. For Skaergaard, the deposit's surface outcrop and near-surface mineralization make open-pit mining the natural starting point, but the decision is more nuanced than simply "dig from the top."

The Case for Open-Pit Mining

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Skaergaard's PGM mineralization outcrops at surface, extending to considerable depth. The upper portions of the deposit are accessible through conventional open-pit methods, which offer several advantages for an Arctic development:

  • **Lower mining cost per tonne**: Open-pit operations typically achieve mining costs of US$2-5 per tonne of ore moved, compared to US$15-40 per tonne for underground operations in similar geological settings
  • **Higher selectivity**: Open-pit mining allows geological teams to see the ore in three dimensions, enabling better grade control and waste rejection during mining
  • **Simpler infrastructure**: Open-pit operations require less complex ventilation, ground support, and materials handling systems than underground mines
  • **Faster startup**: Open-pit construction and commissioning timelines are typically shorter than underground development
  • **Lower capital intensity for initial phase**: A phased open-pit development requires less upfront investment than a full underground mine development

    • The main disadvantage of open-pit mining is the requirement to move large volumes of waste rock relative to the ore recovered. The strip ratio (waste-to-ore ratio) at Skaergaard will increase with depth, eventually making open-pit mining uneconomic.

    The Transition to Underground

    As the open-pit deepens, the strip ratio will increase to a point where underground mining becomes more economical. This transition typically occurs when the pit wall slopes required to maintain stability exceed the practical limits of open-pit design.

    For Skaergaard, conceptual studies have suggested the transition point may be reached at depths of 200-400 meters below surface, depending on pit wall angles, ore geometry, and processing capacity. Underground mining below the open-pit would access deeper portions of the resource that are not economically reachable from surface.

    Underground mining options for Skaergaard include:

  • **Sublevel caving**: A mass mining method suitable for steeply dipping ore bodies where the rock mass is competent enough to allow controlled caving
  • **Longhole stoping**: A method that uses drilled blastholes to break ore, which is then extracted through drawpoints
  • **Room and pillar**: A method used in flat-lying deposits with competent rock, allowing selective mining of higher-grade zones

    • The choice of underground method will depend on detailed geotechnical studies and ore geometry characterization that are part of the advanced feasibility work.

    Phased Development Strategy

    The most likely development approach for Skaergaard is a phased strategy that begins with open-pit mining and transitions to underground in later years. This approach offers several advantages:

  • **Lower initial capital**: Phase 1 open-pit requires US$300-500 million, versus US$500-800 million for a combined open-pit and underground development
  • **Early cash flow**: Open-pit production generates revenue while underground development proceeds
  • **Risk mitigation**: Open-pit operations provide real operational data that reduces uncertainty for underground mine planning
  • **Flexibility**: If metal prices decline or costs increase, the underground expansion can be deferred or canceled without stranding the open-pit investment

    • Arctic-Specific Considerations

    The Arctic environment adds complexity to the mining method decision:

  • **Permafrost**: Skaergaard's location in East Greenland means permafrost may be present at depth, affecting pit wall stability and underground ground conditions. Permafrost degradation during mining could release water and weaken rock masses
  • **Seasonal constraints**: Open-pit mining in the Arctic is constrained by weather, with the operating season typically limited to 6-8 months per year. Underground mining, if developed, could potentially operate year-round since it is less exposed to surface weather
  • **Blasting in frozen ground**: Frozen rock requires different blasting patterns and energy inputs than temperate-zone mining
  • **Waste rock management**: In permafrost environments, waste rock disposal must account for acid rock drainage potential that may accelerate when permafrost thaws

    • Processing Implications

    The mining method also affects processing requirements:

  • **Open-pit ore**: Typically has a higher proportion of oxidized near-surface material that may require different processing conditions than fresh sulfide ore
  • **Underground ore**: Generally presents a more consistent feed grade and mineralogy, but may introduce harder ore that affects grinding circuit performance
  • **Blend management**: During the transition from open-pit to underground, processing the blended ore from both sources requires careful metallurgical management

    • Conclusion

    The mining method decision at Skaergaard is not a binary choice between open-pit and underground. The most likely outcome is a phased development that begins with open-pit mining and transitions to underground as the economics dictate. This approach manages risk while maximizing the resource's potential. The detailed feasibility study will determine the optimal pit depth, underground method, and transition timeline.
