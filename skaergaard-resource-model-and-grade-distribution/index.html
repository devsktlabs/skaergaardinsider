Why the resource model matters for a layered intrusion

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.
A platinum-group metals (PGM) project like Skaergaard is only “bankable” to the extent that its resource model translates into:

  • Spatial confidence (where mineralization is likely to continue)
  • Grade realism (how grades vary vertically and laterally)
  • Process realism (mineralogy-linked recoveries and PGM association)
  • Plan realism (block size, cut-off assumptions, and dilution/schedule)

For a layered mafic intrusion, the geology is not a simple cloud of points. Mineralization is controlled by:

  • Stratigraphy (horizons, reefs, or stratiform units)
  • Local structural offsets and facies changes
  • Textural and mineralogical domains (sulfide content, silicate gangue, PGM host)

That means the modeling workflow must treat stratigraphy as the backbone, then use geostatistics to quantify uncertainty inside those packages.

Building the geological framework first

A competent resource model typically starts with a 3D geological interpretation so that estimates occur inside domains that behave similarly.

Common framework steps used across mafic intrusion resource modeling (and applicable to Skaergaard-type settings):

  • Stratigraphic subdivision
  • Define major units (e.g., basal to upper layered sequence)
  • Identify the mineralized horizons as separate 3D surfaces or volumes
    • Domain definition
  • Delineate mineralization domains based on indicators such as:
  • Sulfide-rich versus silicate-rich layers
  • Different PGM mineral host assemblages
  • Local lithological differences (grain size, alteration, composition)
    • Faulting / warping / folding treatment
  • If structures disrupt stratigraphy, model them explicitly or encode them in the domain model.

    • Why this matters for grade distribution:

    • If you estimate inside the wrong domain, you smear heterogeneity and bias both the mean grade and the tail of high-grade pods.
    • In stratiform systems, grade distribution can be strongly “banded.” The model must preserve that signal.

    Data preparation: assay compositing and uncertainty

    Geostatistics is only as good as the input data preparation.

    1) Assays and key variables

    For PGM deposits, the model often uses multiple variables:

    • PGM grades (e.g., Pt, Pd, Rh) or composite PGM aggregates
    • Gold (Au) when significant for byproduct accounting
    • Base metals (Ni, Cu, Fe) and sulfide proxies when they correlate with PGM mineralization
    • Palladium equivalent (PdEq) and gold equivalent (AuEq)
  • Equivalents are often used in reporting and planning, but the underlying model typically estimates the elemental grades first.

    • 2) Top-cuts and outlier handling

    PGM deposits frequently have:

    • Strongly skewed grade distributions (many low grades, fewer high-grade intervals)
    • Local high-grade intercepts caused by nugget effects and narrow mineralized shoots

    A geostatistical model usually applies:

    • Compositing to a consistent length in drillhole coordinates
    • Top-cuts to reduce undue influence of extreme assays

    The intent is not to “remove value,” but to avoid parameter instability.

    3) Multivariate estimation (often necessary)

    In layered intrusions, Pd, Pt, and Rh frequently co-vary but not identically.

    Common approaches include:

    • Estimating each element separately with correlated variograms
    • Or using multivariate kriging / co-kriging where one variable (e.g., Pd) provides stability and another (e.g., Pt or Rh) follows via covariance structures

    This improves grade distribution fidelity, especially in the tails.

    Variogram design: capturing stratiform continuity

    The key geostatistical concept is continuity, expressed by variograms (or covariance models) in 3D.

    1) Directional continuity

    Layered mafic intrusions tend to show:

    • High continuity along stratigraphy
    • Lower continuity across horizons

    Thus variograms are often anisotropic:

    • Range larger in the stratigraphic direction
    • Range smaller perpendicular to the layers

    2) Measuring spatial scale versus drill spacing

    Even excellent variograms can be undermined by poor sampling density.

    Resource modeling should explicitly match:

    • Variogram ranges to actual sampling
    • Neighborhood search sizes to drillhole geometry

    If the neighborhood is too small, kriging behaves like nearest-neighbor, inflating local extremes. If too large, it over-smooths grade distribution.

    3) Modeling nugget and micro-scale variability

    The nugget effect captures uncertainty at the scale below the composite length.

    In PGM systems, nugget can be high because:

    • Mineralized horizons are thin
    • Assays sample small volumes
    • Mineral hosts can vary at fine scale

    A realistic nugget is essential for producing believable uncertainty and proportion of high-grade blocks.

    Estimation method: balancing smoothness and realism

    For mineral resources, two broad classes are common:

    • Deterministic interpolation (e.g., IDW, nearest neighbor)
    • Geostatistical estimation (e.g., ordinary kriging, kriging with constraints, co-kriging)

    For a technical read, the important question is: does the method preserve stratiform banding and avoid “grade inflation” in the high-grade tail?

    A typical kriging workflow includes:

    • Select variogram model(s) per domain
    • Choose search strategy (minimum/maximum number of samples)
    • Confirm kriging parameters using validation.

    Grade distribution checks: what “good” looks like

    Resource modeling quality is often assessed by comparing:

    • Estimated mean grade vs. declustered sample means
    • Estimated distribution vs. sample distribution
    • Conditioning behavior at drillholes (how close estimates are to composite data)

    Specific grade distribution diagnostics used in practice:

    • Swath plots along stratigraphy and across key horizons
    • Quantile comparisons (e.g., how the 10th, 50th, 90th percentiles behave)
    • Panel/volume validation (comparing estimated grades to withheld data)

    Where grade distribution can mislead mine planning

    Two recurring failure modes:

    1. Over-smoothing

  • Produces a narrow grade distribution
  • Underestimates the fraction of blocks above economic cut-off
  • Can lead to optimistic recovery schedules because grade variability is suppressed

    • 2. Over-fitting / nugget underestimation

  • Produces overly “spiky” block grades
  • Overstates the high-grade tail
  • In practice, reconciliation later shows grade shortfalls or excess dilution needs

    • For cut-off-based reporting, both distortions propagate into stated tonnage and contained metal.

    Linking zones to model outputs (the domain story)

    When investors hear “indicated resource,” they are implicitly asking:

    • How many zones are modeled separately?
    • Are high-grade sub-zones captured as distinct domains?
    • Is continuity directional where geology suggests it?

    In stratiform systems, grade distribution is often zone-dependent:

    • Lower or upper horizons may show different Pd:Pt:Rh ratios
    • Sulfide-rich zones can carry PGM-host minerals more consistently
    • Alteration or brecciation can break continuity, increasing uncertainty and reducing effective ranges

    The best resource models present:

    • Domain geometry and stratigraphic logic
    • Variable selection and transformation
    • Validation outcomes and uncertainty controls

    Interpreting Skaergaard-scale grade distribution in planning terms

    Skaergaard's combined indicated and inferred resource headline (25.4M oz PdEq and 23.5M oz AuEq) indicates large contained value at scale. Translating that into a mine plan requires understanding how PdEq grades are spatially distributed.

    Practical implications for grade distribution interpretation:

    • If mineralization is banded, the plan will be horizon-driven with benches and phases designed to respect those bands.
    • If grade distribution is skewed, cut-off strategy becomes sensitive to:
  • Neighborhood kriging behavior
  • Top-cut decisions
  • Domain thickness assumptions
    • If continuity is anisotropic, block orientation and bench geometries matter.

    What to request in diligence (questions that target grade realism)

    For a technical buyer or analyst assessing a resource model, the most useful diligence questions are:

    • What are the major estimation domains and how are they defined?
    • Are the variograms anisotropic aligned with stratigraphy?
    • What is the role of multivariate estimation (e.g., co-kriging using Pd or base-metal proxies)?
    • How do the validation results compare to declustered sample distributions?
    • What cut-offs were used in defining the reported resource classes?

    Bottom line

    A credible Skaergaard resource model should treat the layered mafic intrusion as stratigraphically controlled, use directional continuity consistent with layered geology, and validate the resulting grade distribution tails that drive cut-off tonnage and mine scheduling.

    When those pieces align, reported combined indicated and inferred resources like the 25.4M oz PdEq and 23.5M oz AuEq can become a controllable production plan rather than a statistical artifact.
