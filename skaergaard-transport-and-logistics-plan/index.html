Skaergaard Transport and Logistics: Getting Metal from Greenland to Market

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardlogisticsshippingtransportarcticgrml

Logistics is one of the defining challenges of Arctic mining. For Skaergaard, everything from diesel fuel to processing reagents must be shipped to Greenland, trucked to the mine site, and consumed in operations. Everything produced, from PGM concentrate to gold, must make the reverse journey. The logistics chain directly affects operating costs, capital requirements, and the project's economic viability.

The Supply Chain

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Inbound logistics to Skaergaard involve several stages:

Ocean transport: Bulk carriers or container ships deliver fuel, equipment, chemicals, and general supplies to a port on the East Greenland coast. Transit times from major supply hubs (Rotterdam, Halifax, Reykjavik) range from 5-15 days depending on origin.

Port operations: The port facility must handle both inbound supply deliveries and outbound concentrate shipments. Natural deep-water harbors in East Greenland's fjord system reduce the need for dredging and breakwater construction.

Haul road: The 60-kilometer road connecting the port to the mine site must support heavy truck traffic year-round. Road construction in Arctic terrain involves permafrost management, stream crossings, and grade control.

Site logistics: On-site material handling, storage, and distribution systems manage the final delivery of supplies to their point of use.

Shipping Season Constraints

East Greenland's shipping season is affected by sea ice, particularly in winter months. The fjord system near Skaergaard's coastal location is generally ice-free year-round due to the East Greenland Current, which carries relatively warm water northward along the coast. However, winter storms and limited daylight can constrain shipping operations.

Practical considerations include:

  • **Year-round access**: The project area's latitude and ocean currents suggest potential for year-round shipping, but with reduced frequency and increased weather risk in winter
  • **Shipping buffer**: An on-site fuel and supply buffer of 2-3 months is prudent to account for weather-related shipping delays
  • **Storage capacity**: The mine site must have sufficient bulk storage for fuel, chemicals, and critical spare parts

    • Concentrate Export

    PGM concentrate from Skaergaard will be shipped as a bulk commodity to overseas smelters and refineries. Key considerations:

  • **Concentrate handling**: Flotation concentrate must be dewatered, filtered, and bagged or loaded into bulk containers for shipping. Moisture content must be controlled to prevent liquefaction during transport
  • **Shipping frequency**: PGM concentrate volumes are relatively small compared to base metal operations. A 300,000 ounce annual PGM production would generate approximately 50,000-100,000 tonnes of concentrate per year, achievable with monthly or bimonthly shipments
  • **Offtake agreements**: Long-term concentrate offtake agreements with PGM smelters (typically in South Africa, Europe, or North America) provide price certainty and logistics coordination

    • Offtake Destinations

    PGM concentrates are refined by a limited number of specialized smelters:

  • **South Africa**: Amplats and Impats operate the world's largest PGM refining complex
  • **Europe**: BASF and Heraeus operate PGM refining facilities in Germany
  • **North America**: Sibanye-Stillwater's Montana smelter is the only US-based PGM smelter
  • **Asia**: Japanese and Chinese refiners process PGM concentrates from various sources

    • The choice of offtake partner affects shipping routes, transit times, and treatment charges. European refineries may offer the most favorable logistics from Greenland, with shorter transit times and established shipping routes.

    Equipment and Heavy Lift

    Mining equipment delivery presents specific logistics challenges:

  • **Mining equipment**: Large mining trucks, excavators, and processing plant components require heavy-lift shipping or disassembly and reassembly
  • **Construction materials**: Cement, steel, and structural materials must be delivered in bulk
  • **Spare parts**: Critical spare parts must be stocked on-site to avoid operational delays from shipping lead times

    • Heavy-lift shipping to East Greenland requires vessels with appropriate crane capacity or port infrastructure to handle heavy cargo. Modular construction techniques can reduce the size of individual components shipped.

    Cost Implications

    Logistics costs are a significant component of Arctic mining operating costs:

  • **Fuel**: US$0.50-1.00 per liter above world market price, reflecting shipping and handling costs
  • **Freight**: Concentrate shipping costs of US$50-100 per tonne depending on destination and route
  • **Supply chain management**: Administrative costs for procurement, customs, and logistics coordination

    • Total logistics costs for Skaergaard are estimated at US$15-25 million annually during operations, representing 5-10% of total operating costs.

    Risk Mitigation

    Several strategies mitigate logistics risks:

  • **Multiple supply sources**: Diversifying fuel and supply sources reduces dependence on any single shipping route or supplier
  • **Inventory buffers**: Maintaining 2-3 month buffers for critical consumables provides resilience against shipping disruptions
  • **Contract logistics**: Long-term contracts with shipping companies provide rate certainty and priority scheduling
  • **Route flexibility**: Alternative shipping routes (via Iceland or northern Europe) provide options if primary routes are disrupted

    • Conclusion

    Skaergaard's logistics chain is complex but manageable. The project's East Greenland location provides year-round marine access, and the 60-kilometer haul road is a proven engineering solution. The primary risks are weather-related shipping delays and fuel cost volatility, both of which can be mitigated through inventory management and contract provisions. Logistics costs, while higher than for projects in established mining regions, are factored into project economics and are offset by the deposit's favorable metallurgy and metal prices.
