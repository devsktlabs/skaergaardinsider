Understanding the geological and technical characteristics of PGM deposits is essential for evaluating development potential. This technical review compares the resource fundamentals of major deposits with a focus on mineralization style, recovery considerations, and development pathway.

The Palladium Problem

Palladium is essential. It's the primary catalyst in automotive catalytic converters, critical for reducing vehicle emissions. It's also used in electronics, dentistry, and chemical processing. And the Western world has a supply problem.

Supply Concentration: The Risk

Global palladium supply is dangerously concentrated:

  • **Russia (~40%)**: Nornickel (Norilsk Nickel) is the world's largest palladium producer. Since 2022, sanctions and geopolitical tensions have made Russian supply unreliable.
  • **South Africa (~35-40%)**: Sibanye-Stillwater, Impala Platinum, and Anglo American Platinum operate Bushveld Complex mines. Political instability, power grid issues (load shedding), deep-level mining costs, and labor disputes create chronic supply uncertainty.
  • **North America (~5%)**: Stillwater (Sibanye) in Montana is the only significant US producer.
  • **Other (~15-20%)**: Zimbabwe, Canada, and minor producers.

    • This means 75-80% of global palladium comes from two jurisdictions with significant political and operational risks. For Western automakers and governments, this concentration is a strategic vulnerability.

    The Demand Side

    Palladium demand is driven primarily by the automotive sector (80%+ of consumption). Despite the electric vehicle transition:

  • Hybrid vehicles still require PGM catalysts (and hybrid sales are growing faster than full EVs in many markets)
  • The existing global fleet of ICE and hybrid vehicles (1.4B+) will need catalyst replacements for decades
  • Hydrogen fuel cells use platinum, but many analysts see palladium substitution potential
  • Industrial and investment demand provide a floor

    • The Deficit

    Multiple analysts project a structural palladium supply deficit through the remainder of the decade. Mine closures (Sibanye's planned restructuring), declining South African output, and unreliable Russian supply are not being offset by new production.

    Skaergaard: A Western Solution

    The Skaergaard Project in Southeast Greenland represents one of the most significant potential contributions to Western palladium supply:

  • **25.4M oz palladium equivalent** — among the largest undeveloped Western PGM resources
  • **NATO jurisdiction** — no sanctions risk, no political instability premium
  • **NI 43-101 compliant** — Western-standard resource reporting
  • **Multiple metals** — palladium, gold, and platinum provide revenue diversification

    • If brought into production, Skaergaard could meaningfully contribute to closing the Western supply gap.

    Other Western PGM Development Projects

    Several projects are advancing, though none match Skaergaard's scale:

  • **Platreef (Ivanhoe Mines, IVN)**: South Africa, first concentrate produced Nov 2025. But SA jurisdiction risk remains.
  • **Marathon (Generation Mining, GENM)**: Ontario, Canada. 2.63M oz Pd proven+probable. Much smaller than Skaergaard.
  • **Waterberg (Platinum Group Metals, PLG)**: South Africa. 63% palladium. SA jurisdiction.
  • **River Valley (New Age Metals)**: Ontario, Canada. Pre-feasibility stage.

    • The Investment Implication

    For investors, the palladium supply deficit creates a compelling case for Western PGM development projects. Projects in secure jurisdictions (Canada, Greenland, Australia) with significant resources and clear development paths are positioned to benefit from:

  • Sustained or elevated PGM prices driven by supply constraints
  • Western government incentives for critical mineral development
  • Automaker interest in securing supply chain diversification
  • ESG-premium valuation for responsibly developed resources

    • Conclusion

    The Western palladium supply gap is real, growing, and not easily solved. Projects like Skaergaard that combine scale, jurisdictional safety, and NI 43-101 compliance are rare. For investors and supply chain strategists, understanding this dynamic is essential.
