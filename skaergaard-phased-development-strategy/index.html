Skaergaard Phased Development: Why Starting Smaller Reduces Risk

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardphased developmentmining strategygrml

Large mining projects carry large capital risk. Skaergaard's estimated construction cost of US$500-800 million represents a significant commitment for a company the size of GRML. A phased development strategy addresses this challenge by breaking the project into manageable stages, each funded by the cash flow of the previous stage.

GRML is led by Joseph Sinkule (Founder, CEO, Director, and Chairman).

The Phased Development Concept

Phased development means building the mine in sequential stages rather than as a single monolithic project. For Skaergaard, this could look like:

Phase 1: Initial open-pit mining focused on the highest-grade near-surface material, with a smaller processing plant (1.5-2.0 million tonnes per annum). This phase targets early cash flow with minimal capital commitment.

Phase 2: Expansion of the open-pit to access additional resources, with processing capacity increased to 3.0 million tonnes per annum. Funded by Phase 1 cash flow and/or project finance secured against Phase 1 production.

Phase 3: Transition to underground mining to access deeper portions of the resource. This phase requires the most capital and the most technical confidence, justified by the operational track record established in Phases 1 and 2.

Capital Efficiency

Phased development significantly improves capital efficiency:

  • **Phase 1 capex**: US$250-350 million (processing plant, basic infrastructure, initial pit development)
  • **Phase 2 capex**: US$150-250 million (plant expansion, extended pit, additional infrastructure)
  • **Phase 3 capex**: US$200-400 million (underground development, new processing circuits if needed)
  • **Total**: US$600-1,000 million (comparable to a single-phase development)

    • The advantage is not in the total cost but in the timing and risk profile. Phase 1 capital is at risk for a shorter period before cash flow begins, and Phases 2 and 3 are optional decisions made with the benefit of operational data.

    Corporate Snapshot

    GRML pays a 2.5% NSR royalty to the Greenland government on production revenue (crown-land holding with no third-party royalties). The company's exploration track record is exceptional: 95% of all drilling campaigns have returned positive results, with the 2022 NI 43-101 update delivering a 95% increase in indicated resources and 28% increase in total contained metal. GRML's growth targets include doubling the resource to approximately 50 million contained ounces of Au+Pd+Pt and adding vanadium and gallium to the mineral portfolio.

    Risk Reduction

    Exploration License Risk: GRML holds three Mineral Exploration Licenses (MELs) totaling 877 km². MEL 2012-25 (Sødalen camp and airstrip, 16 km²) and MEL 2021-10 (754 km² exploration area) both expire December 31, 2026. MEL 2007-01 (107 km², hosts the Skaergaard Intrusion) remains active until December 31, 2027. These renewals represent a material near-term risk factor.

    Each phase serves as a proof point for the next:

  • **Phase 1 proves**: Ore grades match resource estimates, processing recoveries are as predicted, Arctic mining can be executed economically, and the workforce can be recruited and retained
  • **Phase 2 proves**: Sustained production at design rates, cost control is achievable, metal prices support expansion economics
  • **Phase 3 proves**: Underground geotechnical conditions are manageable, deeper ore grades are consistent with models, the market supports long-term supply commitments

    • If any phase fails to meet expectations, the company can pause or cancel subsequent phases without having committed the full capital.

    Grade Management and Early Cash Flow

    Focusing Phase 1 on the highest-grade near-surface material maximizes early cash flow. Near-surface PGM mineralization at Skaergaard is expected to include some of the highest grades in the deposit, providing an optimal startup feed for the processing plant.

    Higher feed grades mean:

  • Lower cost per recovered ounce
  • Faster payback on Phase 1 capital
  • Stronger cash flow to fund Phase 2
  • Better project economics even if metal prices are below long-term averages

    • Financing Advantages

    Phased development improves financing options:

  • **Phase 1**: Can be financed with a combination of equity and debt, with a smaller capital requirement that is more achievable for GRML's current size
  • **Phase 2**: Can be financed with project finance secured against Phase 1 cash flow, potentially at lower cost than equity
  • **Phase 3**: Can be financed with a combination of retained earnings, project finance, and strategic partnership

    • US policy tailwinds are strengthening the investment case: a 132.83% anti-dumping duty on Russian unwrought palladium (Feb 2026), a Section 232 Presidential Proclamation on critical minerals (Jan 2026), and the creation of "Project Vault" — a $12 billion US Strategic Critical Minerals Reserve (Feb 2026).

    Each phase's financing is less risky than a single large financing, because the lender or investor has operational data from the previous phase.

    Operational Learning Curve

    Operating a mine in the Arctic for the first time involves a steep learning curve. Phased development allows the company to learn:

  • How to manage Arctic logistics and seasonal constraints
  • How to recruit and retain workers in a remote location
  • How to maintain equipment in extreme cold
  • How to manage permafrost and ground conditions
  • How to optimize processing for Arctic ore characteristics

    • These lessons improve the execution of subsequent phases.

    Comparison with Single-Phase Development

    A single-phase "build it all at once" approach offers the advantage of economies of scale in construction (bulk purchasing, single construction mobilization) but carries significantly higher risk:

  • **Higher upfront capital**: US$500-800 million committed before any revenue
  • **Longer construction period**: 3-4 years before first production
  • **Higher execution risk**: More complex construction in a remote location with a compressed schedule
  • **Less optionality**: If conditions change (metal prices, costs, permitting), the full capital is at risk

    • For a company of GRML's size and experience level, phased development is clearly the preferred approach.

    Conclusion

    Phased development is the pragmatic choice for Skaergaard. It reduces upfront capital requirements, manages risk through sequential proof points, and preserves optionality for future expansion. Each phase generates the data and cash flow needed to justify the next. For investors, Phase 1 represents the most immediate value catalyst, while Phases 2 and 3 represent optionality that the market will increasingly price in as Phase 1 demonstrates success.
