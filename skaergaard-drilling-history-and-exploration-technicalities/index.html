Skaergaard Drilling History and Exploration Data Quality

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaarddrillingexplorationdata quality

The confidence investors can place in Skaergaard's 25.4 million ounce PdEq and 23.5 million ounce AuEq combined indicated and inferred resource depends entirely on the quality of the underlying exploration data. This article reviews the drilling history, sampling methodology, and data quality that support the resource estimate.

GRML's exploration goals include doubling the resource base to approximately 50 million contained ounces (from the current 23.5M oz AuEq) and expanding the mineral portfolio to include vanadium and gallium.

Exploration History

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

The Skaergaard intrusion has been recognized as a geologically significant feature since the early 20th century, when it was identified as one of the world's best-exposed examples of a layered mafic intrusion. However, systematic exploration for PGM mineralization is a more recent development.

Key phases of exploration activity include:

  • Early geological mapping: Academic and government geological surveys established the basic geology of the Skaergaard intrusion, identifying the layered structure and PGM-bearing horizons
  • Initial exploration drilling: Early-phase drilling programs tested the PGM mineralization identified in surface samples and geological mapping
  • Resource definition drilling: Systematic diamond drilling programs designed to define the resource to indicated category standards, with regular spacing sufficient for geological interpretation
  • Metallurgical drilling: Dedicated drill holes for metallurgical test work, providing representative samples for processing characterization

Drilling Density and Spacing

Resource classification under NI 43-101 and JORC standards requires specific levels of geological confidence, which are achieved through drilling density:

  • Inferred resource: Requires geological continuity that is assumed but not confirmed. Typical drill spacing of 100-200 meters
  • Indicated resource: Requires geological continuity that is reasonably assumed. Typical drill spacing of 50-100 meters with reasonable geological understanding
  • Measured resource: Requires high confidence in geological continuity. Typical drill spacing of 25-50 meters with detailed geological understanding

Skaergaard's combined indicated and inferred resource of 25.4M oz PdEq suggests drill spacing in the indicated category has been achieved across the primary mineralized zones. The quality and consistency of the drilling data directly affects the resource estimate's reliability.

Drilling Method

Diamond drilling is the standard method for PGM exploration, as it provides intact core samples that can be logged, sampled, and assayed with high precision. Key data quality considerations include:

  • Core recovery: High core recovery (above 95%) indicates good drilling practice and provides reliable samples for assay
  • Core orientation: Oriented core allows accurate interpretation of geological structures and mineralization geometry
  • Downhole surveys: Regular downhole surveys ensure accurate knowledge of drill hole trajectories, which is essential for three-dimensional resource modeling
  • QA/QC protocols: Industry-standard quality assurance and quality control procedures, including insertion of blank samples, standards, and duplicates

Sampling and Assaying

PGM assaying is technically demanding due to the nugget effect (coarse-grained PGM minerals that create sampling variability). Skaergaard's sampling methodology should include:

  • Large sample intervals: Longer sample intervals (1-3 meters) help average out the nugget effect
  • Whole-core analysis: For critical intervals, analyzing the entire core (rather than half-core) provides the most representative results
  • Fire assay with ICP finish: Standard analytical method for PGMs, with appropriate detection limits for the expected grade ranges
  • Multi-element analysis: ICP-MS analysis for a full suite of elements, supporting geological interpretation and metallurgical characterization

Resource Estimation Methodology

The resource estimate for Skaergaard should follow industry-standard practices:

  • Geological model: Three-dimensional geological model based on drill hole data, surface mapping, and geophysical surveys
  • Grade interpolation: Ordinary kriging or inverse distance weighting used to interpolate grades between drill holes
  • Block model: Resource estimated on a regular three-dimensional block model, with block sizes appropriate for the drilling density and geological understanding
  • Cut-off grade: Appropriate cut-off grades applied to report resources above economic thresholds
  • Classification: Resource classified as indicated or inferred based on data quality, drill spacing, and geological confidence

Data Gaps and Uncertainties

Every exploration dataset has gaps and uncertainties that investors should understand:

  • Depth extent: The resource may be well-defined to the depth of current drilling but uncertain at greater depths. Underground potential below the planned open-pit is an upside that requires additional drilling to quantify
  • Lateral extent: The margins of the mineralized zones may be less well-defined than the central portions, introducing uncertainty in resource tonnage estimates
  • Grade variability: PGM mineralization is inherently variable, and the nugget effect creates uncertainty in grade estimates between drill holes
  • Metallurgical variability: While metallurgical test work has been conducted, variability in recovery across different ore types and zones adds uncertainty to production forecasts

Independent Verification

For investor confidence, the resource estimate should be prepared or reviewed by a Qualified Person (QP) under NI 43-101 standards. An independent technical report filed on SEDAR provides third-party verification of the resource estimate methodology and results.

Conclusion

Skaergaard's resource estimate is supported by systematic diamond drilling, industry-standard sampling and assaying, and professional resource estimation methodology. The indicated resource category provides reasonable confidence in the deposit's scale and grade. Future drilling to convert inferred resources to indicated, and to test underground potential, will further improve resource confidence and may increase the total resource base. For investors, the data quality supports the resource figures cited in company disclosures and provides a solid foundation for project advancement.
