Skaergaard Power Options: How an Arctic Mine Gets Electricity

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

Power is the lifeblood of a mining operation. Processing plants, ventilation systems, pumping stations, lighting, communications, and camp facilities all require reliable electricity. For Skaergaard, located 60 kilometers from the East Greenland coast with no grid connection, generating power is both a technical challenge and a major cost driver.

Power Demand Profile

A Skaergaard operation processing 3 million tonnes per annum would require approximately 20-30 megawatts (MW) of installed power capacity. The demand profile includes:

  • **Processing plant**: 12-18 MW (grinding mills are the largest single consumers)
  • **Mine equipment**: 3-5 MW (electric shovels, conveyors, pumps)
  • **Camp and facilities**: 2-3 MW (heating, lighting, water treatment, communications)
  • **Infrastructure**: 2-4 MW (port, roads, workshops, warehouses)
  • **Peak vs average**: Mining operations typically run at 60-80% of installed capacity on average, with peak demand during high-production periods

    • Option 1: Diesel Generation (Initial Phase)

    Diesel generators are the default starting point for remote mine power. The advantages are clear: proven technology, rapid deployment, modular scalability, and no dependence on specific site conditions.

    However, diesel has significant drawbacks for an Arctic mining operation:

  • **Fuel cost**: At current diesel prices, fuel cost alone for a 25 MW installation could exceed US$30-40 million annually
  • **Fuel logistics**: Diesel must be shipped to the port and trucked 60 kilometers to the mine site, with seasonal shipping constraints requiring large on-site fuel storage
  • **Carbon intensity**: Diesel generation produces approximately 0.9 tonnes of CO2 per MWh, creating a significant carbon footprint
  • **Maintenance**: Diesel engines require regular maintenance and parts replacement, adding to operating complexity in a remote location

    • Despite the drawbacks, diesel is the most practical option for the initial construction and early operations phase, providing reliable power while longer-term solutions are developed.

    Option 2: Wind Power

    East Greenland has strong and consistent wind resources, making wind power an attractive potential supplement or alternative to diesel:

  • **Resource quality**: Average wind speeds in East Greenland exceed 8-10 meters per second, which is excellent for wind generation
  • **Technology maturity**: Modern wind turbines are proven in Arctic conditions, with installations in Alaska, Scandinavia, and northern Canada
  • **Cost competitiveness**: Wind power has a lower long-term operating cost than diesel, with no fuel requirement

    • Challenges for wind power at Skaergaard:

  • **Intermittency**: Wind power is variable, requiring backup generation for calm periods
  • **Extreme conditions**: East Greenland experiences severe storms and icing conditions that can damage turbines
  • **Seasonal variation**: Wind patterns may not align perfectly with power demand
  • **Grid stability**: A hybrid wind-diesel system requires sophisticated power management to maintain stable supply

    • A wind-diesel hybrid system with 10-15 MW of wind capacity backed by diesel for low-wind periods could reduce diesel consumption by 30-50%, significantly lowering operating costs and emissions.

    Option 3: Hydropower

    Greenland has abundant hydroelectric potential from glacial meltwater rivers. Hydropower offers the most attractive long-term power solution:

  • **Baseload power**: Unlike wind, hydropower can provide consistent baseload power
  • **Low operating cost**: Once built, hydro plants have very low operating costs
  • **Long asset life**: Hydroelectric facilities can operate for 50-100 years with proper maintenance
  • **Low emissions**: Hydropower has minimal greenhouse gas emissions

    • However, hydropower for Skaergaard faces challenges:

  • **Distance**: The nearest glacial rivers may be 20-50 kilometers from the mine site, requiring transmission line construction
  • **Environmental permitting**: Hydro development in Greenland's pristine river systems would face environmental scrutiny
  • **Capital intensity**: Small-scale hydro installations cost US$2,000-5,000 per installed kW, meaning a 20 MW installation would cost US$40-100 million
  • **Construction timeline**: Hydro development takes 3-5 years, making it a long-term solution rather than an immediate option

    • Option 4: Small Modular Nuclear (SMR)

    Small modular reactors are an emerging technology that could potentially provide reliable baseload power for remote industrial operations. However, this option is speculative for Skaergaard:

  • **Regulatory complexity**: Nuclear regulation in Greenland (under Danish authority) would be unprecedented for a mining project
  • **Technology maturity**: SMRs are still in the demonstration phase, with limited commercial deployment
  • **Public acceptance**: Nuclear power faces significant public opposition, particularly in environmentally sensitive Arctic locations
  • **Timeline**: SMR availability for commercial deployment in Greenland is unlikely before 2035

    • SMR is worth monitoring as a future option but is not viable for Skaergaard's near-term development.

    Recommended Power Strategy

    The most practical approach for Skaergaard is an evolutionary strategy:

    Phase 1 (Construction and early operations): Diesel generators provide reliable power while the mine is established. Estimated cost: US$30-40 million in annual fuel expense.

    Phase 2 (Operations optimization): Add 10-15 MW of wind capacity to create a wind-diesel hybrid system. This reduces diesel consumption by 30-50% and lowers annual fuel costs by US$10-20 million. Wind installation cost: US$20-30 million, with a 3-5 year payback at current diesel prices.

    Phase 3 (Long-term optimization): Develop small-scale hydropower if feasible glacial river resources are identified near the project. Hydro would provide baseload power and potentially eliminate diesel dependency. Capital cost: US$40-100 million.

    Cost Comparison

  • **Diesel only**: US$30-40 million/year fuel cost, US$5-10 million/year maintenance
  • **Wind-diesel hybrid**: US$15-25 million/year fuel cost, US$3-5 million/year wind maintenance, US$20-30 million wind capital
  • **Hydro-diesel hybrid**: US$5-10 million/year diesel (backup only), US$2-3 million/year hydro maintenance, US$40-100 million hydro capital

    • The transition from diesel to renewables saves US$15-30 million annually in operating costs, more than justifying the capital investment within 3-5 years.

    Conclusion

    Skaergaard's power strategy should start with diesel for reliability and evolve toward a wind-diesel hybrid within the first years of operation. Hydropower is the ideal long-term solution if a viable glacial river resource can be developed near the project. The evolution from diesel to renewables reduces operating costs, lowers carbon emissions, and aligns with Greenland's environmental standards.
