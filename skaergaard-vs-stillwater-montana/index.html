Skaergaard vs. Stillwater, Montana

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

Why Stillwater is the relevant benchmark

For investors looking for “what does PGM mining look like in production,” Stillwater in Montana is the most visible US reference point.

Stillwater’s significance is practical:

  • It provides operational evidence for PGM ore handling and long-term production economics
  • It demonstrates how processing choices and mineralogy influence recovery stability
  • It anchors expectations for permitting complexity and infrastructure needs in the US context

    • However, Skaergaard and Stillwater are geologically distinct, so the benchmark is not “same ore, different location.” It is “same product class and metallurgical physics.”

    Geology: layered mafic intrusion vs. Stillwater stratiform ores

    Skaergaard is described as a layered mafic intrusion system. Stillwater is also a stratiform system, historically associated with:

  • Layered ultramafic to mafic units
  • PGM-bearing horizons tied to stratigraphy and sulfide mineralization

    • Key comparison points that matter for grade distribution and planning:

  • **Continuity of ore horizons**
  • **Thickness of mineralized units** (influences dilution and cut-off effectiveness)
  • **PGM host mineral assemblages**
  • Hosts affect grind size sensitivity and flotation selectivity

    • Even with similar “layered” language, the specific host mineralogy can differ.

    Mining method and production reality

    Stillwater’s operations historically included underground components with a multi-year schedule and development-driven ramp-up.

    Skaergaard’s stated conceptual mining direction is open-pit planned, phased development.

    This difference drives:

  • **Stripping ratio sensitivity** for early phases
  • **Schedule risk** (open pit can scale with fleet and drilling, but must manage slope and geotechnical constraints)
  • **Grade control demands**
  • Open pit requires tight reconciliation as exposure changes by bench.

    • Stillwater provides evidence that PGM mines can be sustained at scale, but Skaergaard’s open-pit design is a different execution problem.

    Processing: flotation and concentrate-to-refining chain

    PGM projects typically rely on a concentration step and downstream refining.

    The high-level parallels between Stillwater and a Skaergaard-style project:

  • Concentration of PGM-associated minerals into a sulfide-rich stream
  • Metallurgical treatment of concentrate to recover Pd/Pt/Rh

    • Where they diverge:

  • Ore mineralogy controls whether flotation performance is robust at high throughput
  • Silicate versus sulfide partitioning controls grind targets and reagent demand

    • Diligence angle:

  • Stillwater’s historical performance can guide expectations for recovery curves and operational stability
  • But actual Skaergaard testwork must confirm that ore variability does not break the process chain

    • Logistics and infrastructure: Montana vs. East Greenland

    Stillwater benefits from:

  • US industrial logistics
  • Established supply chains
  • Less extreme seasonal constraints than the Arctic

    • Skaergaard’s East Greenland location is characterized by:

  • Remote access dynamics and limited seasonal shipping windows
  • Power and water constraints that can dominate early capex and operating cost
  • Camp and workforce logistics as a core part of execution

    • For a comparison report, the key message is that “technical metallurgy” is only part of total project risk. Infrastructure and climate can move the needle on AISC.

    Cost reality: what benchmarks teach

    Conceptual AISC for Skaergaard is stated as US$800 to 1,100 per PGM ounce.

    Stillwater’s operational data (public reporting over the years) generally shows:

  • PGM mines have meaningful economies of scale
  • Payability and refining costs matter as much as flotation recovery
  • Energy cost and reagent consumption can swing the unit cost

    • Skaergaard’s cost band must be interpreted as an integration of:

  • Recovery and payability assumptions
  • Transport and seasonal logistics costs
  • Power generation and carbon intensity constraints

    • Technology transfer: what to copy, what not to assume

    Copy what is universal:

  • Grade control and reconciliation discipline
  • Concentrate quality control and stability monitoring
  • Maintenance and spares strategies based on metallurgy wear points

    • Do not assume what is ore-specific:

  • Equivalence of host mineral assemblages
  • Recovery stability outside the tested composite set
  • Ramp-up behavior at commercial scale

    • Stillwater’s value is in operational learning: what breaks first, what becomes manageable with operational maturity, and what metrics best correlate with recoveries.

    Permitting and social license: US vs. Greenland governance context

    Stillwater’s permitting path provides a reference for:

  • Baseline environmental data requirements
  • Emissions and water management expectations
  • Stakeholder engagement mechanics

    • Skaergaard must navigate Greenland (Kingdom of Denmark) regulatory expectations and broader geopolitical alignment.

    The technical takeaway:

  • Early environmental baseline collection is not optional; it reduces uncertainty and litigation risk.
  • Long-term water treatment and tailings governance are central.

    • Bottom line

    Stillwater (Montana) is the best US proof point that PGM ores can be operated through a concentration-to-refining chain and sustained at industrial scale.

    Skaergaard’s open-pit phased approach, East Greenland logistics, and layered intrusion metallurgy are different execution challenges. The correct use of Stillwater is benchmarking operational discipline and cost drivers, not assuming identical geology-driven metallurgy.
