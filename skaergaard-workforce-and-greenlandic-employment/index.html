Skaergaard Workforce Strategy: Greenlandic Employment and Arctic Mining Labor

By Dr. Henrik Larsen · 2026-03-23

skaergaardworkforcegreenland employmentlaborgrml

One of the most significant challenges for any Arctic mining development is building a workforce. Greenland has a small population of approximately 57,000 people, limited mining industry experience, and no existing mining workforce near the Skaergaard site. The workforce strategy will shape the project's social license, operating costs, and long-term sustainability.

Greenland's Labor Landscape

Greenland's workforce has several characteristics relevant to mining development:

  • **Small population**: Approximately 57,000 people spread across a vast territory, with most concentrated in the southwest around Nuuk and Sisimiut
  • **Limited mining experience**: The Nalunaq gold mine (operated 2004-2013) was Greenland's only large-scale modern mining operation, and many of its workers have moved on to other careers
  • **Fishing and public sector dominance**: The Greenlandic economy is dominated by fishing and public sector employment
  • **Language**: Greenlandic (Kalaallisut) is the primary language, with Danish as the administrative language and English used in international business
  • **Education**: Greenland has a technical school and a university, but specialized mining training programs are limited

    • The Fly-In-Fly-Out Model

    Most remote Arctic mining operations use a fly-in-fly-out (FIFO) workforce model, and Skaergaard will almost certainly follow this approach. Under FIFO:

  • Workers fly to the mine site from their home communities (or from Denmark, Iceland, or Canada)
  • Typical rotations are 2 weeks on, 2 weeks off, or 4 weeks on, 2 weeks off
  • The company provides accommodation, catering, and recreational facilities at the mine site
  • Workers are transported by aircraft, with the company chartering flights or using existing air services

    • FIFO has several advantages for Skaergaard: it avoids the need to build a permanent mining town (reducing infrastructure cost), allows workers to maintain their homes and families in existing communities, and provides access to a broader labor pool than the local area alone could supply.

    Greenlandic Hiring Targets

    The Greenland government will expect Skaergaard to prioritize Greenlandic employment as a condition of the operating permit. Typical expectations for mining projects in Greenland include:

  • **Minimum Greenlandic employment**: 30-40% of the workforce during operations, with targets for increasing this percentage over time
  • **Training investment**: Commitment to training Greenlandic workers for skilled positions (heavy equipment operators, process plant operators, maintenance technicians)
  • **Local procurement**: Priority for Greenlandic suppliers and service providers where available
  • **Community benefit**: Contributions to local community development, education, and infrastructure

    • Meeting these targets requires a deliberate workforce development strategy that begins well before operations start.

    The Training Challenge

    Building a skilled mining workforce from a population with limited mining experience is a multi-year effort:

  • **Pre-operations training**: 1-2 years before first production, the company should begin training programs for key operational roles
  • **Partnerships with technical schools**: Collaborating with Greenland's technical education institutions to develop mining-specific curricula
  • **On-the-job training**: Hiring Greenlandic workers for construction roles provides hands-on experience that transfers to operations
  • **International mentorship**: Pairing experienced mining professionals with Greenlandic trainees accelerates skill development
  • **Apprenticeship programs**: Formal apprenticeship structures that provide structured career pathways

    • Salary and Benefits

    Arctic mining operations must offer competitive compensation to attract workers to a remote, harsh environment. Skaergaard's compensation package will need to include:

  • **Base salary**: Premium over comparable positions in temperate locations, reflecting the hardship of Arctic conditions
  • **Rotation bonuses**: Additional compensation for time spent at the remote site
  • **Travel provision**: Flights to and from the mine site provided at company expense
  • **Accommodation and meals**: Provided at no cost during rotations
  • **Health and safety**: Comprehensive medical coverage and on-site medical facilities
  • **Retirement and savings**: Competitive benefits packages

    • The compensation premium for Arctic mining is typically 20-40% above comparable positions in North America or Europe, adding to operating costs but necessary for workforce attraction and retention.

    Retention in Isolation

    Worker retention in isolated Arctic operations is a recognized challenge. Long rotations, separation from family, limited recreation options, and harsh weather all contribute to turnover. Mitigation strategies include:

  • **Modern camp facilities**: High-quality accommodation, reliable internet, recreation areas, and private rooms improve the remote living experience
  • **Mental health support**: Counseling services and peer support programs address the psychological challenges of isolation
  • **Family-friendly rotation schedules**: Rotations that balance operational needs with family time improve long-term retention
  • **Career development**: Clear advancement pathways motivate workers to stay with the company

    • Economic Impact on Greenland

    Skaergaard's workforce will have significant economic impact on Greenland:

  • **Direct employment**: 200-500 full-time positions during operations, plus construction workforce
  • **Indirect employment**: Supply chain, transportation, catering, and services create additional jobs
  • **Skills transfer**: Mining skills developed at Skaergaard transfer to future projects, supporting Greenland's broader mining sector
  • **Wage income**: Mining wages are significantly above Greenland's average income, increasing household spending and government tax revenue

    • Conclusion

    Workforce development is both a challenge and an opportunity for Skaergaard. The challenge is building a skilled mining workforce in a location with limited mining experience. The opportunity is creating lasting economic benefit for Greenland through employment, training, and skills development. A well-executed workforce strategy strengthens the social license to operate and positions Greenland for broader mining sector growth.
